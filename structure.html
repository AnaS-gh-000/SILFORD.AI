HERBAL IDENTIFICATION MADE EASY

Nature is full of plants that look remarkably alike, but some harmless herbs have dangerous toxic counterparts. Our AI-powered plant identification tool helps you quickly distinguish between safe and potentially toxic plants from a simple photo.

About Us

Silford arose from a vision. We envisioned a tool that could help people confidently identify plants, reducing the risk of confusing harmless species with toxic lookalikes and making outdoor exploration a little safer.

Driven by that vision, we built an AI-powered platform that combines modern machine learning with botanical knowledge to identify plants from a single photograph.

Our Mission

Our mission is to empower hikers, gardeners, foragers, students, and nature enthusiasts with accessible technology that supports informed decisions.

Our Model & Its Working

01. Image Capture

Upload an existing image or take a live photo of the plant.

02. AI Feature Analysis

A Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) with Localized Attention focuses on key visual regions such as leaf shape & stem texture.

03. Safety Assessment

The identified plant is compared with visually similar toxic and non-toxic relatives, helping users avoid dangerous misidentifications.

04. Detailed Results

Each prediction shows a detailed result with plant name, close toxic relatives, key features, & more.

FOXGLOVE

Scientific name:
Digitalis purpurea
Family name:
Plantaginaceae
TOXICITY:

Highly Toxic.

Potentially fatal

SYSTEMS AFFECTED:

  • Cardiovascular/ Circulatory system
  • Nervous system
  • Digestive system

Not to be ingested or touched; all parts are highly toxic and can lead to severe poisoning.

Key Constituents:

  • Digoxin (Cardiac glycoside)
  • Digitoxin (Cardiac glycoside)
  • Digitalin
  • Gitoxin (Glycoside)
  • Gitaloxin (Glycoside)
  • Flavonoids
  • Saponins
  • Anthraquinones

Effects on body:

Gastrointestinal Effects

  • Severe Nausea and intense feeling of sickness.
  • Violent Vomiting that resists standard medication.
  • Abdominal Pain and severe cramping.

Cardiovascular Effects

  • Extreme Bradycardia where pulse drops dangerously low.
  • Irregular Pulse, heart skips beat or feels fluttering.
  • Hypotension, blood pressure dropping sharply, causing fainting.

Toxic Parts:

Leaves

Contain the highest concentration of toxins, primarily digitoxin, digoxin, and gitaloxin.

Seeds

Highly concentrated with digitalin and other active glycosides, can be absorbed through skin.

Flowers & Buds

Contain significant amounts of gitoxin and digitoxin, along with toxic saponins.

All parts of the foxglove plant (Digitalis purpurea) are highly toxic and potentially fatal if ingested or handled improperly.

Handling safety:

  • Wear protective clothing to avoid skin contact with sap.
  • Use thick rubber or gardening gloves that are waterproof.
  • Protect face, nose, and mouth while handling the plant. Do not touch face.
  • Always wash hands, forearms thoroughly with soap after handling.
  • Do not burn any part of the plant. Can lead to respiratory poisoning.

Commonly mistaken for:

Highly Toxic

Delphinium

Spurred, five-petaled flowers instead of foxglove's specked bells.

Not Toxic

Penstemon

Has smaller, narrower open-mouthed tubes in airy clusters.

Mildly Toxic

Lupine

Winged, pea-like flowers forming a stiff, pointed cone.

Mildly Toxic

Comfrey

Bears tiny clusters of fused bells on foliage, lacks foxglove's erect stems.

Medicinal uses:

In modern medicine, raw plant is never used directly due to its extreme toxicity. Instead, pharmaceutical companies extract specific chemicals from the plant for use.

Congestive Heart Failure (CHF)

Digoxin is prescribed to manage symptoms in patients with mild-to-moderate heart failure.

Atrial Fibrillation and Flutter

It is used for ventricular rate control in patients with chronic atrial fibrillation. It stabilizes the heart.

Note: There is a very narrow difference between medicinal doses and toxic doses.

If touched
